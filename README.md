As generative AI systems become increasingly capable of reproducing art, writing, and poetry, the ability for humans to distinguish between the two becomes less clear. 
This project aims to explore the evolving relationship between humans and artifically crafted art, with a focus on AI and its growing place in creative spaces.
The rapid development of AI has raised critical questions about the historical functions art has had for humanity. 

If art has functioned as such a deep form of emotional expression, what does it mean if this expression can be replicated or even preferred to be replicated by artificial systems?

Recent research suggests that this boundary is already destabilised, with studies showing that the modern audience is usually unable to distinguish between AI-generated poetry and human-written work and in some cases favour AI work in terms of rhythm,
clarity and emotional accessibility. 
Creating a paradox of artificial work being seen as more 'human'; which directly challenges assumptions about creativity and authenticity that we know.
(https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-76900-1)

Our project aims to respond to this tension through an interactive infinite-zoom experience into the human body. 
Separated into 6 layers: skin, muscle, organs, vessels, skeleton, and ultimately the soul, they represent both physical anatomy and metaphorical depth with
transitions between layers designed to feel continuous rather than segmented, reinforcing the “zoom” concept. As the user progresses inward, via scrolling with their mouse, t
hey will encounter fragments of poetry distributed across these layers. Some of these texts are human-written, while others are generated by AI and sometimes in binary itself
furthering the idea of deliberately blurring authorship and encouraging reflection on the origin and meaning of creative expression.

We aim to interrogate the idea that creativity, being inherently part of the human experience, can be simulated through data and pattern recognition. The final 'soul' layer serves as a conceptual endpoint

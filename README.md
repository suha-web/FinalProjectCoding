As generative AI systems become increasingly capable of reproducing art, writing, and poetry, the ability for humans to distinguish between the two becomes less clear. 
This project aims to explore the evolving relationship between humans and artifically crafted art, with a focus on AI and its growing place in creative spaces.
The rapid development of AI has raised critical questions about the historical functions art has had for humanity. 

If art has functioned as such a deep form of emotional expression, what does it mean if this expression can be replicated or even preferred to be replicated by artificial systems?

Recent research suggests that this boundary is already destabilised, with studies showing that the modern audience is usually unable to distinguish between AI-generated poetry and human-written work and in some cases favour AI work in terms of rhythm, clarity and emotional accessibility.  Creating a paradox of artificial work being seen as more 'human'; which directly challenges assumptions about creativity and authenticity that we know.
(https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-76900-1)

Our project aims to respond to this tension through an interactive infinite-zoom experience into the human body. 

Artistic direction, Gavriel:

Originally designed to be split into 6 layers: skin, muscle, organs, vessels, skeleton, and ultimately the soul, they were to serve to represent both physical anatomy and metaphorical depth with transitions between layers designed to feel continuous rather than segmented, reinforcing the “zoom” concept(which was then cut down to four as the inclusion of the Nervous system and Vessel network was cut out, was reconsidered. While these systems would have increased anatomical completeness, they began to conflict with the project’s aim of making a clear progression from physical structure to abstract interior. Such highly complex systems such as the nervous network risked overwhelming the visual composition with its density making it difficult to maintain a strong focal point (the central opening), and could have disrupted the directional flow.) As the user progresses inward, via scrolling with their mouse, they will encounter fragments of poetry distributed across these layers to lend to the idea of emotions and expression of them being generated internally. Some of these texts are human-written, while others are generated by AI and sometimes in binary itself furthering the idea of deliberately blurring authorship and encouraging reflection on the origin and meaning of creative expression.

We aim to interrogate the idea that creativity, being inherently part of the human experience, can be simulated through data and pattern recognition. The final 'soul' layer serves as a conceptual endpoint and is drawn by hand with no words to influence or distract the viewer from the abstract presentation. 

Our central visual motif is the opened torso, which acts as both a literal and symbolic entry point. Inspired by anatomical imagery and historical representations of the dissected body, rather than functioning purely as biological realism; the human form is presented not as an identifiable individual, but as an anonymous silhouette, functioning as a universal structure, and shared, collective experience.

A combination of photobashed anatomical elements and digital painting was used to construct the internal layers. This was an intentional choice as the photographic elements (e.g. organs, skeleton) introduce a sense of realism and discomfort and contrast the painted and abstract elements, disrupting this realism and deterring the piece from appearing completely medical.

This tension reflects the project’s conceptual focus on AI. Just as AI-generated content imitates reality without fully embodying human experience, the visuals sit between accurate representation and pre-constructed collages. Another key artistic decision was the inclusion of both human-written and AI-generated poetry, without explicitly distinguishing between them within the experience.

The human-written poems (e.g. Salita S. Bryant, Sylvia Plath, Carol Ann Duffy) were selected for their focus on fragmentation and medical or anatomical imagery, themes that align directly with the visual dissection of the body as poetry is historically understood as a uniquely human form of expression, tied to lived experience and the subjectivity of that. (https://isrf.org/blog/ai-poetry-and-the-human-writing-subject)

The AI-generated poetry (e.g. I Am Code) was integrated alongside these works to introduce ambiguity, however, with an intention to destabilise the viewer’s ability to assign authorship of which is which as opposed to contrast overtly.
